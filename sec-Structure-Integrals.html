    Section 3.2 The Structure of Integrals

    In the Great Gorilla Jump problem, you saw that we can figure out the total distance traveled by adding up a bunch of pieces of distance together, and we obtained each little piece of the distance by multiplying the respective velocity and the elapsed time. This is exactly what an integral is, that we are adding a bunch of the pieces together obtained by multiplying something together. This idea is called "Adding-up-Pieces".
    ðŸ”—
    In this activity, you will explain what each component in the Great Gorilla Jump problem represents and then generalize it to explore the structure of the definite integrals.
    ðŸ”—
    P.S.: This idea may seem similar to the Riemann sum you learned back in section 5.2. This is perfectly correct! The Adding-up-Pieces idea is like a generalized version of the Riemann sum in the sense that we donâ€™t only apply this idea to just finding the area of a bunch of rectangles or the area under the curve.
    ðŸ”—

    Exercises Part (a): Math up the Great Gorilla Jump Problem!

    Letâ€™s call \(\Delta t\) the elapsed time (and it is typically 0.5 seconds) and \(v(t_i)\) the velocity of the gorilla at a certain point, where \(i\) is the index to indicate which piece we are referring to. If we call each piece of the distance \(d_i\text{,}\) then it can be obtained by
    \begin{equation*} d_i = v(t_i)\Delta t \end{equation*}
    You also found out that we can find the total distance by adding up the pieces together. By using the fancy sigma notation you learned before, the total distance traveled is
    \begin{equation*} d \approx \sum_{i = 1}^N d_i = \sum_{i = 1}^N v(t_i)\, \Delta t \end{equation*}
    where \(N\) denotes the number of pieces.
    ðŸ”—
    We know that the approximation is better and better if we recorded more and more velocity as that will force the elapsed time to become smaller and smaller. Symbolically speaking, the total distance travel can be computed by
    \begin{equation*} d = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^N v(t_i)\, \Delta t \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Now letâ€™s switch our attention to the definite integrals. Hypothetically speaking letâ€™s say we knew the velocity function, \(y = v(t)\text{.}\) Then by section 5.6, we know that the total distance traveled is
    \begin{equation*} d = \int_0^5 v(t)\, dt \end{equation*}
    Note that there is no turnaround so the displacement and the distance traveled should be the same (if we are less picky about the sign).
    ðŸ”—
    Wow thatâ€™s a long preamble..., and here is your task! We have two expressions to represent the same thing (the total distance) so we can equate them.
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^5 v(t)\, dt = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^N v(t_i)\, \Delta t \end{equation*}

    Solution.
    The symbol "\(\int\)" is just a fancy cursive S that represents Summation. The \(0\) and the \(5\) are the initial and the final value of the time in this context.
    ðŸ”—
    The \(v(t)\) here denotes the velocity of the gorilla at a certain timestamp \(t\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The \(dt\) here represents the elapsed time where the gap is infinitesimal small (so \(dt\) is just a fancy way of representing \(\Delta t\) as the gap approaches \(0\)).
    ðŸ”—
    The \(v(t)\, dt\) is a representative of one piece of the distance (we multiplied the velocity at time \(t\) with the elapsed time \(dt\)), and the \(\int\) symbol tells us to add all the pieces of distance together to obtain the total distance traveled.

    Exercises Part (b): General Structure of Integrals

    The general structure of integrals is like the one below (you can find a similar version in our textbook in section 5.2):
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^N f(x_i) \, \Delta x \end{equation*}
    From the Great Gorilla Jump problem, we got the distance by (1) multiplying, and then (2) adding. By staring at the structure of a generic definite integral, \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dt\) ,what is it that are being multiplied and what is it that is being added? Which symbol (or the lack of symbol) represents the multiplication and which symbol represents the addition?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Similar to the gorilla problem, we multiply the integrand with the differential together. That is, we multiply \(f(x)\) with \(dx\text{.}\) The lack of symbol between these two things represent multiplication (this is the juxtaposition in math, that the lack of symbols between two things represents multiplication).
    ðŸ”—
    After the multiplication, the \(f(x)\, dx\) represent one piece of the quantity we are looking for. The integral symbol, \(\int\text{,}\) is telling us to add all of the pieces together, while the limits of integrations, \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) tells us where to start and where to end.
    ðŸ”—
    This is essentially what integration means, that we are adding up infinitely many pieces together, where each piece is obtained by multiplying two quantities together.
